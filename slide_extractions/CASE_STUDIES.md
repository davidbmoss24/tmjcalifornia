# Case Studies from "The Broad Health Implications of Oral Degeneration"

Source: Dr. Jennings' lecture slide deck — same content in both `Jaw slides, condensed.pdf` and `weston price lecture 2 25-djlaptop.pptx`.

These four cases are presented in the deck as **visual / imaging cases** — each is built from cephalometric X-rays, panoramic dental X-rays, and CBCT (cone-beam CT) cross-sections of the temporomandibular joints. The deck contains very little narrative text on the case slides; what we have here comes from (1) what's visible in the images, (2) the speaker notes Dr. Jennings embedded in the PowerPoint, and (3) the diagnostic framing the surrounding lecture provides.

> Most of these cases would benefit from Dr. Jennings adding patient context (age, presenting complaint, treatment course, outcome). The visual material is strong; the narrative is what's currently missing for website use.

---

## Case I — TMJ compression driven by a deviated septum

**Imaging shown in the deck:**
1. Lateral cephalometric X-ray of the skull (full profile of head, neck, airway, and jaw position)
2. Panoramic dental X-ray showing the full upper and lower arches
3. CBCT cross-sectional series of the temporomandibular joints — multiple coronal and sagittal slices through both TMJs

**Speaker note (from Dr. Jennings):** "TMJ compressed, flattened, bent. Deviated septum on right causing left TMJ compression."

**What this case demonstrates:**
The patient's right-side deviated nasal septum has structurally pushed the head/jaw mechanics off-center. The downstream consequence is **compression of the left TMJ** — visible in the CBCT slices as flattening and bending of the condyle. This is a textbook example of Dr. Jennings' contention that airway anatomy (specifically the nose) is part of jaw orthopedics: when one side of the airway is blocked, the developing facial structure shrinks on that side, and the lower jaw gets pushed into the skull on the opposing side. The case is shown to illustrate why ENT findings and TMJ findings cannot be evaluated in isolation.

**Suggested narrative for website use** _(needs Dr. Jennings' confirmation on patient specifics)_:
> "Multiple specialists could not explain this patient's symptoms. Imaging revealed that a right-sided deviated septum — a structural finding that had been considered cosmetically irrelevant — was driving compression of the left temporomandibular joint. Treatment focused on jaw repositioning to relieve the joint, not on the septum itself."

## Case II — Successful treatment with resulting underbite and "military neck"

**Imaging shown in the deck:**
1. Lateral cephalometric X-ray — post-treatment, shows the new jaw alignment
2. Panoramic dental X-ray (labeled with the imaging lab's mark — "Restón Diagnostics" — visible at the top of the image)
3. CBCT cross-sectional series of both TMJs

**Speaker notes (from Dr. Jennings):**
- "Treated case, now underbite, military neck"
- "Short condyle"
- "Small right condyle"

**What this case demonstrates:**
A patient brought into proper jaw position by Dr. Jennings' protocol. The treatment endpoint left this patient with a slight **underbite** — which is the correct functional position for this anatomy (recall: 10% of patients, per Dr. Jennings' clinical experience, properly should bite underbit). The patient also displays a "military neck" — loss of normal cervical curvature, which often resolves further as the jaw repositioning continues to influence head posture over time. CBCT findings document the residual structural issues — a short and small right condyle — that the jaw orthopedic appliance is compensating for.

**Suggested narrative for website use**:
> "Not every successful case ends with a textbook overbite. For some patients, the functional position of the jaw is forward of where dentistry's structural model would place it. Imaging here shows a treated patient whose post-treatment position is a slight underbite — the alignment that's biomechanically right for this anatomy, even though it's the opposite of what conventional orthodontics would produce."

## Case III — Airway compromise and joint pathology in detail

**Imaging shown in the deck:**
1. Color-mapped (orange-overlay) skull CT — a lateral 3D rendering highlighting the bone and soft-tissue relationships
2. CT mid-sagittal section with the **upper airway / oropharyngeal space highlighted in red** — making the constricted airway visually unmistakable
3. Four CBCT cross-sections of the TMJ — each showing the joint condyle in a different slice (millimeter-by-millimeter detail of joint shape)
4. Four close-up bony detail images of the condyle / joint structures
5. Profile clinical photograph showing the post-treatment teeth alignment in vivo

**What this case demonstrates:**
The most thoroughly documented case in the deck. The highlighted-airway image is the standout — it shows visually how a retruded jaw narrows the airway behind the tongue, which is the mechanism Dr. Jennings cites for the high prevalence of obstructive sleep apnea in modern populations. The series of joint cross-sections walks the viewer through the pathology slice by slice, demonstrating that the TMJ damage is not subtle on imaging — it's measurable in millimeters.

**Suggested narrative for website use**:
> "This case is a teaching case. When you can see the airway behind the tongue in red — and how dramatically it narrows when the jaw is retruded — it stops being abstract. The same patient's CBCT cross-sections show the consequences for the joint: flattened, displaced, mechanically compromised. None of this was identifiable on a standard 2D dental X-ray."

## Case IV — Airway compromise with treated bite

**Imaging shown in the deck:**
1. CBCT mid-sagittal section showing the head in profile with **the airway again highlighted in red** — another striking demonstration of airway compromise
2. Four CBCT TMJ cross-sections showing condyle morphology
3. Color clinical photograph of the patient's mouth/teeth in profile — visible bearded male, showing the bite alignment in life

**What this case demonstrates:**
A second airway case paired with TMJ pathology — likely chosen to show that the airway-narrowing finding from Case III is not an outlier. The matched clinical photograph of the patient's actual mouth grounds the imaging in a real, recognizable human face. This is the kind of pairing — imaging finding plus real-life "this is the patient" photograph — that the new website would benefit enormously from if the patient consented to its use.

**Suggested narrative for website use**:
> "Cases III and IV are deliberately presented together: two different patients, both with the airway behind the tongue narrowed by the position of the lower jaw. This pattern is what Dr. Jennings sees again and again in patients arriving with snoring, sleep apnea, and the long downstream consequences — fatigue, brain fog, cardiovascular strain — that obstructive sleep apnea drives over time."

## The "for want of a nail" framing (Slides 33–34)

The case studies are bookended by a memorable rhetorical device — Dr. Jennings adapts the classic English proverb to make his thesis vivid:

> *For want of a nail, the shoe was lost; For want of a shoe, the horse was lost; For want of a horse, the rider was lost; For want of a rider, the battle was lost; For want of a battle, the kingdom was lost; And all for the want of a horseshoe nail.*

His version:

> *For want of convenience, our food was tossed.*
> *For want of food, our bite was flossed* [pron. "lost"].
> *For want of a bite, our health was lost.*
> *For want of health, we search at great cost.*
> *All for the want of convenience.*

The slide that follows ("Summary") closes with the three-line indictment of the field:

> - The human condition has severely degenerated.
> - The degeneration is not recognized by either the medical or the dental communities (orthodontics aligns teeth, not jaws).
> - Dentists are responsible for the TMJ, but they don't image it. Why?

This is one of the most quotable passages in any of his material and a strong candidate for direct use on the new site (with attribution).

## Recommended next step

The cases are visually powerful but narratively thin. Before they go on the new site, Dr. Jennings should provide for each:

1. **Patient context** — age, sex, presenting complaints (de-identified)
2. **Prior workup** — what other specialists had examined the patient, what they found or missed
3. **Treatment course** — how long, what appliance(s), what was hardest
4. **Outcome** — what changed, how it was measured, how long-lasting
5. **Consent** — explicit written consent to use the imaging and any clinical photographs on a public-facing website

The four case-history templates from the Silicon Valley Health Institute lecture (Chris Coffield, Adriana Finney, Philippi with macular degeneration, plus the one written case on the old site — the man who fainted on uphill walks) are good narrative models. The PowerPoint imaging cases would pair powerfully with that narrative voice.
