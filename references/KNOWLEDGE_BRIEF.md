## 1. The Trigeminal Nerve

The fifth cranial nerve (CN V). The largest cranial nerve, responsible for **all sensation** of the face, scalp (front two-thirds), mouth, nose, sinuses, teeth, gums, dura mater (the membrane around the brain), and the temporomandibular joint, plus **motor control** of the muscles of mastication (chewing) and a few smaller muscles: tensor tympani (an ear muscle that dampens loud sounds), tensor veli palatini (which opens the Eustachian tube), mylohyoid, and the anterior belly of the digastric.

The nerve has three branches:
- **V1 / Ophthalmic** — sensation from the eye and forehead
- **V2 / Maxillary** — sensation from the cheek, upper lip, upper teeth
- **V3 / Mandibular** — sensation from the lower jaw and lower teeth, plus motor control of the chewing muscles

The trigeminal nerve cell bodies (the *trigeminal ganglion*) sit in a depression of the temporal bone called Meckel's cave. From there, the central axons project into the brainstem and synapse in the **trigeminal nuclear complex** — which extends from the midbrain down to about cervical level C2–C3.

**The mesencephalic nucleus is anatomically unique.** Almost all sensory cell bodies in the body sit in peripheral ganglia *outside* the central nervous system (think dorsal root ganglia for spinal sensation, the geniculate ganglion for taste, etc.). The mesencephalic nucleus of the trigeminal — which receives proprioceptive input from the chewing muscles and the periodontal ligaments around the teeth — is the only sensory nucleus whose cell bodies are *inside* the brain itself. Dr. Jennings cites this fact repeatedly; it's accurate, and it's a useful piece of evidence for his "the jaw has unique access to the brain" framing.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ The trigeminal handles facial sensation and chewing motor control. The mesencephalic nucleus is the unique-in-the-CNS exception for primary sensory cell bodies. The trigeminal nuclei extend down into the cervical spinal cord, sharing space with sensory input from C1–C3.
- _Framing:_ The claim that the trigeminal "has 100× more dense pain fibers than any other nerve" — Dr. Jennings repeats this consistently. The published literature certainly describes the trigeminal as densely innervated with nociceptive fibers (this is part of why a face slap, an eye irritation, or a jaw injury produces such intense pain). The exact "100×" multiplier is not a number we can pin to a single citation; treat it as his clinical paraphrase of the qualitative fact rather than a precise published statistic.

## 2. Substance P

A short peptide neurotransmitter — 11 amino acids — first isolated in 1931, structure determined in 1971. Belongs to the **tachykinin** family of neuropeptides. Encoded by the TAC1 gene on chromosome 7. Binds primarily to the **NK-1 receptor** (Neurokinin-1).

Substance P is secreted by nerves (particularly the unmyelinated C-fiber pain afferents) and by a number of inflammatory cells: macrophages, eosinophils, lymphocytes, and dendritic cells.

### What it actually does

Substance P is a **pro-inflammatory signaling molecule** with effects that are now well-documented across multiple body systems. From the peer-reviewed review O'Connor et al., *Journal of Cellular Physiology*, 2004 (PMID 15334652) — the paper Dave Asprey and Dr. Jennings cited as the cornerstone reference:

> "SP has proinflammatory effects in immune and epithelial cells and participates in inflammatory diseases of the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and musculoskeletal systems."

Specifically documented in that paper and the surrounding literature:

- **Respiratory:** Asthma patients are hyperresponsive to substance P. NK-1 receptor expression is increased in their bronchi. Substance P participates in virus-associated respiratory infection, non-productive cough, allergic rhinitis, sarcoidosis.
- **Gastrointestinal:** Regulates smooth muscle contractility, epithelial ion transport, vascular permeability, immune function. Elevated levels and upregulated NK-1R found in the rectum and colon of patients with **inflammatory bowel disease**, correlating with disease activity.
- **Musculoskeletal:** Elevated levels found in synovial fluid and serum of patients with **rheumatoid arthritis**. NK-1R mRNA upregulated in RA synoviocytes.
- **Fibromyalgia:** Spinal fluid substance P levels are 3–5× normal — this is a well-documented and replicated finding.
- **Pain:** Substance P is the canonical pro-nociceptive neuropeptide. It's released from peripheral nerve endings when tissue is damaged and from central terminals in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord.

### What raises and lowers it

- **Raises:** Chronic activation of C-fiber pain pathways (which is Dr. Jennings' proposed mechanism for jaw dysfunction → systemic substance P elevation), allergens, irritants, stress.
- **Lowers / blocks:**
  - **Capsaicin** (from chili peppers) is the canonical substance P depleter. Repeated capsaicin exposure exhausts substance P stores in sensory neurons — the basis for topical capsaicin pain creams.
  - **Aprepitant (Emend)** — an oral NK-1 receptor antagonist. FDA-approved for chemotherapy-induced nausea and post-operative nausea. Studied off-label for many other conditions including depression and inflammatory pain. Roughly $200–$300 per pill, as Dr. Jennings says.
  - **Glucocorticoids** decrease NK-1R expression in epithelial and inflammatory cells and increase production of NEP (neutral endopeptidase), the enzyme that degrades substance P. This is part of why steroids work for inflammation.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ Substance P is pro-inflammatory and is documented in asthma, IBD, RA, fibromyalgia, migraine, depression. Aprepitant exists and is FDA-approved (currently only for the narrow indications above). Capsaicin depletes substance P. Glucocorticoids degrade it.
- _Framing:_ Dr. Jennings' claim that substance P is the **sole** cause of leukemia or **the primary driver of most cancer** — this is one researcher's framing that is *not* mainstream. There are individual papers linking elevated substance P to tumor progression (it's known to modulate hematopoiesis), but "substance P causes most cancer" is not an established medical position. Be careful with the cancer claims in patient copy; the safer framing is "substance P appears to play a role in tumor biology and is an active area of research."

## 3. C Fibers and Polymodal Nociceptors

C fibers are one of three classes of nerve fiber (along with A and B). The key facts:

- **Unmyelinated** — they lack the fatty myelin sheath that surrounds most other nerve fibers, which is why they conduct signals slowly (~1–2 m/s vs. 5–120 m/s for myelinated A fibers).
- **Small diameter** — ~0.2–1.5 μm.
- **Carry sensory information** to the central nervous system, primarily pain and temperature.
- **Include postganglionic autonomic fibers** as well as dorsal root sensory fibers.

The clinically important subset is the **C polymodal nociceptor**. From the *Progress in Brain Research* review by Belmonte and Cervero (PMID 9009726):

> "The cutaneous sensory units labeled C-fiber polymodal nociceptors have a broadly coherent set of responsive characteristics. These include: (a) elevated thresholds to mechanical stimulation and to heat; (b) excitation by irritant and algesic chemicals; and (c) sensitization by injury or algesic substances."

Polymodal means they respond to **multiple types of stimuli** — mechanical pressure, heat, cold, and chemical irritants — converging through the same nerve fiber. This is well-established neuroscience.

**Dr. Jennings' polymodal claim** goes further: he tells audiences that C fibers also pick up electromagnetic fields, radiation, barometric pressure, and body pH. The published peer-reviewed literature on polymodal nociceptors describes chemical, mechanical, and thermal sensitivity (including chemical detection of pH — that's well-documented; tissue acidity activates these nerves, which is part of how ischemic pain works). The EMF and radiation parts are not standard polymodal-nociceptor science; this appears to be Dr. Jennings extending the framework to integrative areas where it hasn't been formally tested.

**Vanilloid receptors.** Capsaicin (from chili peppers) and other "burning" sensations activate **TRPV1**, which used to be called the "vanilloid receptor 1" because vanillin was the prototype molecule. This is real and well-documented. It's why Dr. Jennings can say all your spices act on a related receptor family — turmeric (curcumin), cinnamon (cinnamaldehyde), and vanilla all interact with TRP-channel chemistry.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ C fibers are unmyelinated and slow. C polymodal nociceptors respond to mechanical, thermal, chemical (including pH) stimuli. Capsaicin activates TRPV1 (a vanilloid receptor) and depletes substance P. Vanilla, cinnamon, turmeric act on TRP-channel biology.
- _Framing:_ The claim that C fibers detect EMF, ionizing radiation, and barometric pressure as part of normal polymodal function — this extends beyond the standard nociceptor literature. Dr. Jennings appears to be drawing on integrative-medicine literature on EMF biology that doesn't have the same evidentiary base as the core polymodal-nociceptor science. Use cautiously in patient copy.

## 4. The Trigemino-Vascular System

This is the mechanism behind migraine — well-documented and the basis of an entire class of FDA-approved migraine drugs (triptans and CGRP-targeted monoclonals).

From the *Pathologie Biologie* review (PMID 1379707) Dr. Jennings cited:

> "Neurogenic inflammation has been proposed as a possible pathogenetic mechanism for migraine and cluster headache. Antidromic stimulation of trigeminal fibers causes plasma protein extravasation, mast cell activation and degranulation… in rat dura mater."

The chain of events in a migraine:

1. Trigeminal sensory fibers that innervate the blood vessels of the dura mater (the membrane around the brain) become activated.
2. They release **substance P, CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide), and neurokinin A** at their peripheral nerve endings.
3. This causes blood vessel dilation, plasma protein leakage, mast cell activation — collectively "neurogenic inflammation" in the brain's blood vessels.
4. Pain signals travel back along the trigeminal to the brain.
5. Migraine.

Triptans (sumatriptan, etc.) work by activating 5-HT1B/1D serotonin receptors on those sensory nerve endings, blocking the release of the inflammatory neuropeptides. Newer drugs (erenumab, fremanezumab, ubrogepant) specifically target CGRP and its receptor — a generation of migraine prevention that has emerged in the last decade.

This is *the* mechanistic story behind why Dr. Jennings' framework links bite dysfunction to chronic headaches. If the trigeminal nerve is chronically hyperexcited (from a misaligned jaw forcing the chewing muscles to overwork), it has a lower threshold for triggering this whole inflammatory cascade. That's why he says bite alignment therapy is 85% effective at reducing headaches "regardless of the type of headache" — at the mechanism level, most chronic headaches share this final common pathway.

**Brain blood flow caveat.** Dr. Jennings frequently references trigeminal control of "regional cerebral blood flow" — the brain freeze / boxer's KO mechanism. There is real published work on trigeminal-mediated cerebral vasoconstriction; trigeminal stimulation does affect cerebral vessels acutely. The strongest evidence is in the dura mater (which is what migraine drugs target). Whether *chronic* low-grade trigeminal activation drives chronic cerebral hypoperfusion in real-world patients is a more speculative extension and isn't as well established.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ The trigemino-vascular system is real and is the established mechanism for migraine. Substance P, CGRP, and neurokinin A are released. Triptans and anti-CGRP drugs are direct therapeutic confirmations of the model.
- _Framing:_ Chronic low-grade trigeminal activation causing chronic cerebral hypoperfusion is plausible but not as strongly evidenced as the acute migraine version. The "40% reduction in brain blood flow in people missing molars when they clench" claim refers to a Japanese study Dr. Jennings cites; the specific paper is not in my fetched references and should be verified before publication.

## 5. The Neural Crest

A transient embryonic structure unique to vertebrates. The neural crest forms during the third and fourth weeks of human gestation, alongside the closing neural tube. Its cells then migrate throughout the developing embryo and differentiate into a strikingly diverse set of adult tissues.

Cells derived from the neural crest include:

- **Sensory neurons** of the peripheral nervous system (dorsal root ganglia, cranial nerve ganglia)
- **Autonomic neurons** (sympathetic and parasympathetic)
- **Enteric neurons** — the entire "gut brain" (~500 million neurons)
- **Schwann cells** — the myelin-producing cells of peripheral nerves
- **Adrenal medulla** chromaffin cells (which produce adrenaline)
- **Melanocytes** — the pigment cells of the skin, hair, and inner ear
- **A large portion of the bones and connective tissue of the face**, including:
  - The bones around the eyes and ears
  - **Most of the bones of the jaw and palate**
  - **Dentin (the main tissue of teeth)** and parts of the periodontal ligament
  - The four **front (incisor) teeth** in particular are heavily neural-crest derived
- **Smooth muscle of the aortic arch arteries**
- Parts of the eye (cornea, iris stroma)

This is the **embryological reason** the front four teeth, the autonomic nervous system, the melanocytes, and the trigeminal sensory neurons are linked in Dr. Jennings' framework. They share a developmental origin, so a perturbation that affects neural-crest-derived structures during development tends to affect *multiple* of these tissues at once — and disturbances downstream of those tissues (like chronic trigeminal activation from a misaligned jaw) might preferentially impact other neural-crest-derived systems.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ The neural crest gives rise to all the structures listed above. This is foundational developmental biology, taught in every embryology textbook. Front teeth, peripheral autonomic neurons, melanocytes, and trigeminal sensory neurons do share this developmental origin.
- _Framing:_ The clinical implication that a misaligned bite preferentially damages neural-crest-derived tissues is Dr. Jennings' theoretical extension. The pigmentation cases (vitiligo reversal, freckle resolution) he reports are clinical anecdotes, not peer-reviewed outcomes — striking, but not the kind of evidence base that lets us say "bite work cures vitiligo" without qualification.

## 6. Myelin and Why C Fibers Are Different

Most nerve fibers are wrapped in **myelin** — a fatty insulating sheath produced by Schwann cells (in the peripheral nervous system) or oligodendrocytes (in the central nervous system). Myelin lets nerve impulses skip along the fiber rapidly (~120 m/s in the fastest fibers — that's why you yank your hand off a hot stove before you consciously "feel" the heat).

C fibers don't have it. Myelin-loss diseases like **multiple sclerosis** demonstrate what happens when myelination fails: progressive motor and sensory dysfunction, dramatic increases in pain sensitivity, fatigue, and characteristic neurological deficits.

The key takeaway for Dr. Jennings' framework: the slow, unmyelinated C fibers are the substrate of chronic pain. They're what carries the long-lasting, dull, aching, hard-to-localize pain that characterizes chronic conditions. Fast A fibers carry sharp, well-localized, momentary pain. When the trigeminal system becomes chronically irritated, it's the C-fiber pathway that drives long-term substance P elevation and downstream inflammation.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ Myelination biology and the A-fiber vs. C-fiber distinction is textbook neuroscience.
- _Framing:_ Nothing speculative here — this is straight anatomy and physiology.

## 7. TMJ Dysfunction — Mainstream Position

The Wikipedia entry on temporomandibular joint dysfunction (which has good citations) reflects the consensus view: TMJ dysfunction is a **multifactorial condition** with no single agreed cause. Contributing factors include occlusal abnormalities, bruxism, joint inflammation, trauma, psychological stress, and possibly genetic predisposition. Estimated prevalence: 5–12% of the general population in some form, with women diagnosed about twice as often as men. The current mainstream treatment hierarchy:

1. **Conservative reversible**: education, soft diet, jaw rest, NSAIDs, physical therapy, stress management
2. **Occlusal splints / mouthguards** — used widely; mainstream view is they reduce muscle activity and protect from grinding rather than "correcting" anything
3. **Pharmacotherapy** — muscle relaxants, antidepressants for chronic pain, sometimes anticonvulsants
4. **Trigger-point / botulinum injections** — for myofascial cases
5. **Arthrocentesis / arthroscopy** — for internal derangement
6. **Open joint surgery** — last resort

Notably, the mainstream literature treats **occlusal correction / jaw repositioning as controversial** — many academic dental sources actively warn against irreversible occlusal therapy for TMJ. This is the position Dr. Jennings spends much of his career arguing against.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ TMJ dysfunction is multifactorial and prevalent. Standard care is conservative-first.
- _Framing:_ The whole positioning of Dr. Jennings' practice is a deliberate challenge to the mainstream conservative-first paradigm. He believes precision jaw orthopedics — using removable appliances to actually reposition the jaw and rebuild back-tooth height over months — is the missing curative treatment. This is a minority view in academic dentistry. The new website should be confident about presenting his framework while being honest that it sits outside the mainstream paradigm. (That's actually a *feature*, not a bug, for patient acquisition — the patients who find him are the ones the mainstream paradigm has failed.)

## 8. The Parkinson's / TMD Link

Dr. Jennings published his hypothesis paper with the Parkinson's Resource Organization in June 2014: *Cranio-Mandibular Dysfunction (TMJ) Causation Theory of Parkinson's*. The full text is preserved at `references/raw_text/parkinsons-causation-theory.txt`. Key claims:

- Over half the population has significant CMD.
- Jaw recession over the last ~20,000 years has driven a parallel rise in airway obstruction and CMD-related neurology.
- **Emma Thornton's 2008 doctoral thesis** showed substance-P antagonists could reverse experimental Parkinson's in animal models. This is the key piece of supporting laboratory evidence Dr. Jennings cites.
- He uses Occam's razor: since CMD can plausibly account for **every** Parkinson's symptom (motor, blink reflex, smell loss, sleep, cognitive, autonomic, bone loss), and since some patients have reversed Parkinson's-like presentations with jaw therapy, CMD should be considered a primary suspect.

His specific symptom-by-symptom mechanism (drawn from the article):

| Parkinson's symptom | CMD-mediated mechanism |
| --- | --- |
| Motor (bradykinesia, tremor, rigidity) | Trigeminal proprioceptors → reticular formation (where movement originates). Substance P modulates movement. Japanese animal studies — lowering bite causes scoliosis and rigidity. |
| Decreased brain blood flow | Trigemino-vascular system. Cited study: clenching with missing molars → 40% drop in brain blood flow. |
| Elevated substance P | Trigeminal C fibers are the primary source of substance P. |
| Sleep problems | Trigeminal modulates the reticular activating system. |
| Reduced sense of smell | Two noses — olfactory (CN I) and trigeminal chemoreceptors (CN V). |
| Rhinorrhea, allergies, sinus | Autonomics to sinuses travel with trigeminal sensory fibers. |
| Retinal alterations | Trigeminal sensory innervation of the eye. |
| Accelerated bone loss | Substance P modulates bone metabolism. |
| Blink reflex defects (70% of Parkinson's patients) | Trigeminal is sensory limb of the blink reflex. |
| Cognitive impairment | Regional cerebral blood flow + substance P neuromodulation. |
| Autonomic disturbances | Animal studies — bite destruction reliably produces autonomic dysfunction. |

### The peer-reviewed view as of 2026

The strongest peer-reviewed evidence on the link is the Chen et al. 2019 paper in *PLoS One* — a population-based study using Taiwan's National Health Insurance data, ~6,000 PD patients matched to ~18,000 controls. Headline finding:

> "We used Cox proportional hazard regression models to calculate the relative risk of TMD and found a **2.11-fold (95% CI: 1.35–3.30) increased risk of TMD overall in the PD group** compared with the non-PD group. … there was a **4.25-fold increased risk in the PD group in the first year** after the initial PD diagnosis and a 3.88-fold increased risk in the second year."

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis (Minervini et al., *Journal of Oral Rehabilitation*) reached compatible conclusions: TMD prevalence is meaningfully elevated in Parkinson's populations.

**Crucially**, the mainstream peer-reviewed framing is **association, not causation**. The standard interpretation is that PD pathology (rigidity, tremor, bruxism, drug side effects) damages the TMJ — the causality runs PD → TMD. Dr. Jennings argues the reverse: CMD → PD. The peer-reviewed data is **temporally** consistent with either direction.

The Chen paper notes one interesting finding: TMD risk is elevated *most* in the first 1–2 years after PD diagnosis, then declines somewhat. Dr. Jennings would interpret this as PD being detected in patients whose pre-existing CMD has been driving symptoms for years; the conventional interpretation is that early PD treatment medications cause acute TMD.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ TMD prevalence is roughly 2× elevated in Parkinson's populations (peer-reviewed). Substance P antagonists can reduce experimental Parkinson's-like behavior in animal models (Emma Thornton 2008, and a fair amount of follow-on literature).
- _Framing:_ The causal direction (CMD → PD) is Dr. Jennings' hypothesis. Mainstream interpretation runs the other way (PD → TMD). The clinical case reports of Parkinson's-like symptoms reversing with jaw therapy are individually striking but not systematically replicated in controlled trials.

For website copy: it's defensible to say "Parkinson's patients have approximately twice the rate of TMJ dysfunction in peer-reviewed population studies — and Dr. Jennings has spent four decades developing the clinical hypothesis that, for many of these patients, the jaw dysfunction came first." That's accurate to both literatures.

## 9. The Lovett Brothers Principle (cervical / lumbar correspondence)

I couldn't fetch the specific reference Dr. Jennings cited (the URL is on a wellness-blog site that wasn't responsive at the time of writing). But the principle itself is well-known in the chiropractic and osteopathic literature: there's a paired functional relationship between upper-cervical vertebrae and lumbar vertebrae such that a torsional dysfunction at one end of the spine tends to produce a compensatory pattern at the other. **C1 ↔ L5, C2 ↔ L4, C3 ↔ L3,** etc. The original work was by physical therapist Robert W. Lovett in the early 20th century. It is *not* mainstream medical / neurological doctrine, but it's standard within chiropractic practice and is cited fairly often in osteopathic manual medicine literature. Dr. Jennings uses it to explain why his bite-realignment patients often see resolution of low-back pain — fixing the jaw stabilizes upper cervical, which translates through the Lovett correspondence to the lumbar spine.

**What's solid vs. what's framing**
- _Solid:_ A real principle within chiropractic / osteopathic manual medicine.
- _Framing:_ Not in mainstream spine surgery or orthopedic literature.

## 10. Professional Bodies / Where to Refer

**ICCMO — International College of Cranio-Mandibular Orthopedics.** Founded 1980. The credentialing body for the precision jaw orthopedics specialty Dr. Jennings practices. His MICCMO designation is from this organization (the "Master" level — most senior). Their "Find a Member" directory at iccmo.org is the resource he points patients to for clinicians outside the Bay Area.

**AACP — American Academy of Craniofacial Pain.** Founded 1985. Publishes *TMDiary*, the journal in which Dr. Jennings' 2008 Parkinson's hypothesis paper appeared. Broader scope than ICCMO — includes any clinician treating orofacial pain conditions.

Both organizations are real, both maintain referral directories, both should be linked from the new site's "Find a Specialist" or "Referrals" page.
