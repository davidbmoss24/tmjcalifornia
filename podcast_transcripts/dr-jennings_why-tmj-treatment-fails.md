**What is TMJ?**

TMJ stands for *temporomandibular joint dysfunction*. Some call it TMD (temporomandibular disorders). It's a collection of symptoms relating to pathology in and about the jaw joint. Common problems:
- Clicking and popping in the jaw joints
- Headaches
- Tooth pain
- Sinus dysfunction, post-nasal drip
- Visual disturbances
- A wide spectrum of other symptoms

**The pathology found in the vast majority of TMJ cases:**

The back teeth are not developmentally long enough, leading to the jaw joint compressing up into the skull too far. This makes the musculature that compresses the joint work too hard, which affects the neurology, which has broad medical implications.

On a normal joint you want the space behind the condyle to be twice as big as the space in front. With time, the jaw goes back, gets squished forward, rotates — and every time you open, it pops back into position; every time you close, it gets squished out of position. That's the classic TMJ clicking, popping, jaw locking.

**Why most TMJ treatment fails:**

1. **Wrong understanding of structure, anatomy, physiology, function.** Many dentists think TMJ is psychosomatic and treat it with biofeedback, stress management, physical therapy — never correcting the structural elements.

2. **Wrong treatment approach.** Treatment requires getting the jaw down and forward — putting mouthpieces on the back teeth to make them taller.

3. **Wrong jaw position chosen.** In Dr. Jennings' office, they use speech and opening/closing arc to determine where the jaw should go. Have someone open wide, bite on a stick — and as they close on that stick, the jaw will naturally go to the right position about 90% of the time.

4. **Wrong appliances.** Many dentists use flat mouthpieces. Flat mouthpieces don't work. You need something that interdigitates the jaw very precisely in a forward position — needs grooves.

5. **No nighttime appliance.** When you lie down, fall asleep, lose muscle tone — the jaw will displace. That recompression at night excites a strong neurological response that brings back symptoms.

6. **Not maintained over time.** This is a four-dimensional problem — the jaw is off vertically, laterally, front-to-back, AND in time. When you support the back teeth, the jaw decompresses and wants to be down a little further over time. You have to keep adjusting these mouthpieces so contact is only on back molars. If you hit forward, the jaw has to pull back to engage the back teeth, stressing the neurology.

7. **No long-term retention.** After treatment, the patient needs a night appliance that maintains the jaw position while they're asleep — otherwise any clenching, biting, etc., will cause the back teeth to become re-intruded and you end up where you started.
