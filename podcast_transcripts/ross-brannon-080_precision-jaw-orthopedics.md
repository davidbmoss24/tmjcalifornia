This episode is uniquely targeted at *other dentists* — Dr. Jennings explaining what he does and why few in the profession do it.

**On precision jaw orthopedics:**

90% of Dr. Jennings' practice is precision jaw orthopedics: placing patients on a relaxed trajectory of opening and closing, doing anterior repositioning therapy, balancing into pivoting mechanics where they hit molars stronger than anteriors for condylar decompression. The goal is to get the mandible on a relaxed trajectory that decompresses the TMJ.

Braces are designed to give a cosmetic look — they're not designed to orthopedically reposition the mandible, don't incorporate TMJ or airway considerations.

How many dentists are aware of what he's doing? Very few. "A dozen or less."

**On the trigeminal system and brain — most evocative explanation:**

All dentists know about the trigeminal nerve. Nobody knows about the *trigeminal system* — how this nerve interfaces with the brain.

The trigeminal proprioceptors (jaw alignment sensors) are the only sensory cell bodies in the brain. Developmentally, humans have a massive amount of focus on jaw alignment because of that simple fact. Those sensors in the brain have:

1. Massive feed into the reticular formation (reptilian brain, center of sensory integration). When jaw alignment is off, it alters reticular formation tonicity → people get processing errors, movement disorders (Tourette's, dystonia, scoliosis, torticollis, Parkinson's).

2. Adjacency to the locus coeruleus (sympathetic ganglion). When the bite's off, you go into sympathetic dominance — constantly anxious. "You're not going to get anywhere on opioid addiction if you don't look at bites. These people are all self-medicating because of this anxiety and unease."

**On why precision matters:**

"How you hit on the last tooth in the back is more important than all the rest put together. And it has to be really, really precise or they'll get into symptoms."

Most dental cases aren't precise enough. When orthodontic cases are finished, they have a nice aesthetic look but in the mouth they're off a millimeter here and a millimeter there.

Dr. Jennings uses polyvinyl siloxane (silicone bite) to look at how the posterior molars are aligned right-to-left. Very precise cusp-fossa alignment. Carbon paper isn't precise enough.

**On medical history correlation:**

Dr. Jennings' patients typically present with a generalized hypersensitivity syndrome — sensitive to noise, light, hot, cold, food, drugs, stress. The kind of sensitivity that causes a lot of imbalance, upsets homeostasis. They're sick frequently within a broad spectrum of medical complaints.

"When you're diagnosed with allergies, does anybody ever tell you why you have the allergies? No. Nobody looks at substance P. The literature is very clear — 26,000 articles on PubMed. Very extensively studied since 1980. But nobody's integrated it because there's no pharmaceutical remedy."

**On TMJ:**

Temporomandibular joint dysfunction: jaw joint in front of the ear develops painful conditions. People can have covert dysfunction without TMJ symptoms — Dr. Jennings looks at functional level, not just diagnosis.

Typically the jaw's back too far. The meniscus inside the joint gets squished forward. Every time they open it pops back where it's supposed to be; every time they close it gets squished forward again. That's the clicking, popping, grappling noises.

The two largest TMJ organizations in the U.S. recommend anterior repositioning therapy. Other schools still think it's psychosomatic (treated with biofeedback, stress management, physical therapy). The psychosomatic schools don't get the structural elements corrected.

When you do anterior repositioning therapy properly — decompress the joint, recapture the disc, support the bite precisely — symptoms go away. Joint noise eliminated. Facial pain gone.

**Treatment timeline:**

Most cases: 15-18 months. Cases with prior extracted teeth that have to be reopened orthodontically can take 3-5 years.

**On dental school's failures:**

"They [orthodontists] don't agree with my occlusal position — what they've been taught in school is to leave that slight overbite." A recent survey of orthodontists: 70% don't think the bite has anything to do with TMJ.

Schools teach an aesthetic model, not a functional model. Dr. Jennings: "Are the schools teaching the wrong things? Yes."

"The biggest challenge is — it's easier just to do implants and cavities and all that other fun stuff. It's just a lot easier and more profitable and less education required."

Cancer, substance P, asthma — all underappreciated by mainstream medicine because there's no pharmaceutical remedy for substance P.

**On the future and where the field is going:**

Growing interest. Several medical-dental organizations created in the last decade. But still a small field. Long learning curve. Treacherous because hypersensitive patients will react if you don't treat precisely. Different appliances than traditional TMJ.

Airway is driving more dentists into this work, but they're still treating airway only at nighttime, not in the daytime. They're not assessing where the jaw needs to be functionally.
