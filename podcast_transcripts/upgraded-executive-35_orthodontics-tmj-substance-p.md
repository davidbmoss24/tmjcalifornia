**Mechanism — trigeminal system into the brain:**

The trigeminal nerve is sensory from ear to ear and here to the crown of your head. All dentists and medical people know about the trigeminal nerve, but what nobody seems to know about is the trigeminal *system* — how this nerve interfaces with the brain. There are proprioceptors (sensors in your teeth, gums, and tissues) that tell the brain where your jaw is in space. They go to a very special nucleus in the brain called the trigeminal mesencephalic, which is composed of the only sensory cell bodies in the brain. All your sensors for your eyes, ears, and skin are outside in the ganglia, but your trigeminal sensors are in the brain. They have a massive influence on the sympathetic system and on the reticular formation — your reptilian brain and center of sensory integration. When the bite is off, sensory processing gets disturbed.

There's research by an Australian researcher in 1964 showing that the predominant neurons in your reticular formation are second-order neurons coming from your trigeminal mesencephalic.

Trigeminal mesencephalic is paired side-by-side with your locus coeruleus, your sympathetic ganglion in the brain — so it has a major influence on sympathetic tone. With jaw dysfunction you often see anxiety disorders, excess worrying, panic attacks, fight-or-flight responses.

The trigeminal nerve also controls brain blood flow — that's why when a boxer gets hit in the jaw he passes out. Stimulate this nerve too much and you get a brain freeze. Clinically it manifests with brain fog, slow brain function, mental acuity issues, developmental disabilities.

**Substance P range of effects:**

Substance P opens up cell membranes — the membrane becomes porous. The cell membrane potential drops, and cells fire too easily. Substance P is implicated in:
- Allergies, asthma, autoimmune disorders, hypersensitivity syndromes
- Most forms of cancer (including pancreatic — 80+ articles on PubMed)
- Mood disorders, anger, violence, addiction, PTSD, depression, anxiety
- Eczema, seborrhea, psoriasis, acne
- Fibromyalgia (substance P 3-5× normal in spinal fluid)
- Chronic fatigue, mitochondrial dysfunction
- IBS, Crohn's
- Migraines and chronic headaches
- Seizures
- Movement disorders (Tourette's, Parkinson's, dystonia, scoliosis)

**Clinical screening — the popsicle stick test:**

Most people will close to where their jaw functionally wants to be 95% of the time if you just stick a popsicle stick or tongue blade between their front teeth and have them close. That's accurate enough to determine where the jaw needs to be. Dentists don't know this.

Other tests:
- Put your little finger in your ear and pull forward. You should NOT be able to feel your jaw joint open and close. About 20% of the population, the jaw joint severely impacts back into the ear space.
- Look in a mirror and check whether you bite and talk on the same trajectory. Most people with bad bites thrust the jaw forward when talking to control airflow. The jaw should bite and talk on the same trajectory.
