A short, very focused explanation of the Parkinson's / TMJ link.

Dr. Jennings: I'm Dr. Dwight Jennings, here at our clinic Northern California Craniofacial Center. We treat a wide variety of medical conditions through jaw orthopedics. Today we're going to be talking about Parkinson's and how Parkinson's is related to jaw alignment dysfunction.

**Two anatomical findings in Parkinson's patients:**

1. **The jaw joint is compressed too far back in the skull.** Normally you'd like the whole lower jaw to be more forward, with the space behind the condyle larger than the space in front, where a little disc fits between the jaw and the skull.

2. **They're all "a-gape"** — their jaw falls too far away from the upper teeth in rest, slightly open. Often this makes their voice sound more hollow or gives them a little lisp.

**Four mechanisms connecting jaw alignment to Parkinson's symptoms:**

1. **Rigidity.** The Japanese have done 35 years of research showing that anytime the teeth are shortened on one side of the body, it induces increased hypertonicity in the muscles on the opposite side. Very strong compelling evidence that rigidity can be induced by a bite being off.

2. **Abnormal blink reflex.** Found in 70% of Parkinson's patients. The blink reflex is modulated by your trigeminal nerve (sensory in) → seventh facial nerve (motor out). With bite dysfunction, the trigeminal sensory is disturbed in a high percentage of cases, altering blink reflex.

3. **Loss of smell.** There are two "noses" — the olfactory (first cranial nerve) and the chemoreceptors controlled by the trigeminal. With trigeminal disturbance, you often see shifting in smell characteristics — from multiple chemical sensitivity all the way to loss of smell. Dr. Jennings has had many cases where therapy restored smell after many years of being missing.

4. **Movement origination.** They've known since 1905 that stimulating the reticular formation (the reptilian part of the brain, lower part of the brain) induces tremors and parkinsonian-type movements. Research shows your jaw alignment sensors go through the trigeminal into the trigeminal mesencephalic nucleus, then send second-order neurons into the reticular formation. In fact, those are the predominant, most populous cells within the reticular formation — second-order neurons from your jaw alignment sensors.

**Treatment:** an orthotic that covers up the teeth and fills up the a-gape-ness, restoring jaw alignment.
