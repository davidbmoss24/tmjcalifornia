## Lecture (Dr. Jennings)

"I graduated in 1976 from dental school in San Francisco. Shortly thereafter I got into European-type orthodontics. Since 1986 my practice has been limited to orofacial pain and orthodontics on a full-time basis."

**The faces of primitive humans:**

"As you look at faces — faces are a very important focus. A lot of my theories also get back into primitive skulls and primitive bites. I developed theories based on what we call biomechanical principles of occlusion, which aren't within the understanding of dentistry. All primitive skulls had an end-on-end bite. They didn't have overbites as modern man has. Combined with neuromuscular dentistry (which came out of Seattle in the early '70s using computerized jaw motion analysis), these two principles led us to look at arc of motion. The main treatment protocol these days in our patients is to get them so they open and close on a fair arc. 90-95% of patients end up with an end-on-end bite. Some cases end up with a class III with the lower jaw further forward."

**The trigeminal system in detail:**

"The trigeminal nerve has 100 times more dense pain fibers than any other nerve in your body. That leads it to be the predominant sensory input into the brain. C fibers transmit their signal via substance P. Substance P is not a neurotransmitter — it's a neuropeptide. Neuropeptides linger in the body and have endocrine-like properties.

"Your temporalis is your primary jaw-posturing muscle. The primary pathology in TMJ problems is that your jaw is back too far. This muscle is working too hard. People get headaches in the temporal area all the time. The temporalis is also the predominant influence on gallbladder function. In Chinese medicine, the gallbladder meridian starts here and crisscrosses through the temporalis a couple of times. When you're chewing, this muscle tells the gallbladder 'get rid of bile — food's coming.' In TMJ this is overactive — you get a lot of gallbladder removals with TMJ problems."

**Biomechanical principles of occlusion (the key claim):**

"Dentistry historically looked at teeth to figure out where the jaw needs to be. Neuromuscular dentistry figured out you have to make the muscles happy too. Teeth aren't the only thing — you have to integrate muscle information. Teeth are part of your skeletal system. You have to have a musculo-skeletal balanced system."

**Open-and-close arc, and substance P sequelae:**

"When your bite is bad, the trigeminal becomes hyperactive and hypertonic. As trigeminal tonicity goes up, you start getting brain processes affecting homeostasis, autonomic, cognitive, motor function. Substance P elevation systemically follows."

**Why bites are degenerating (long-term Q&A response):**

"Most people that I treat, the jaw is retruded 3mm — that's where they start getting substantial symptoms. Some cases see 5-6mm retrusion. Some get symptoms at 1-2mm. Right around 1/16 inch retrusion."

**Skin disorders:**

"Most skin disorders on the body are related to substance P levels. Substance P is known to be associated with most skin disorders. As your bite goes off, substance P levels go up, you get systemic skin disorders."

**Eye diseases:**

"Very unexplored area. Brain research showing over-stimulation of the trigeminal will cause the skin on the surface of the eyeball to grow scaly — dry eye feeling when you have adequate tears. I believe eventually they'll find glaucoma, macular degeneration, and other eye diseases are also related, because the only sensory to that eyeball is from the trigeminal."

**Ear infections:**

"Tensor velli palatini — a little muscle that opens and closes your eustachian tube. Tensor tympani — a little muscle in your ear that dampens noises. When tonicity is elevated in the trigeminal, you get chronic ear infections, depressed hearing, tinnitus. **A couple of studies on 3-5 year olds with chronic ear infections: when they glued plastic on the baby teeth and built them up higher (decompressed the joint, lengthened those muscles), in both studies they were about 95% effective at stopping all future ear infections.**"

**ADD / ADHD / fibromyalgia / chronic fatigue:**

"The reticular activating system — your reptilian brain — is heavily trigeminal modulated. PET scans and SPECT scans show blood flow alterations in ADD problems are going to be heavily trigeminal modulated. Few are exploring this, but you can modulate ADD through the trigeminal.

"Neural crest cells form your mesencephalic nucleus, all the cranial nerves, sympathetic/parasympathetic nerves, the enteric plexus that goes to your gut. That's why you find this connection with jaw dysfunction and gut issues."

**Limbic / pneumonia / immune function (these are some of his strongest empirical claims):**

"There's a book — *The Dental Physician* — talking about lots of cases of repetitive pneumonia that went away when he fixed the bites. Lots of cases of mononucleosis where white blood cell counts normalized routinely within 2-4 hours of building up bites. Substance P alters the shape of red blood cells — research showing he could alter shape within about a week's period of time with splint therapy."

**Calcium metabolism / non-union ankles:**

"I had a case of 40 broken bones by age 18 — when we fixed his bite, he had 2 major falls in 5 years and never had another broken bone. Told he had a genetic disorder. **I've had 5 cases of non-union ankles that never healed until we fixed the jaws.** People had tried stimulators, bone grafts, everything. In laboratory animals, when you throw the bites off, their mineral levels go wacko. The trigeminal has the ability to modulate nutrient levels in the blood — proteins, fats, sugars. Hypoglycemic symptoms are a common finding with jaw dysfunction."

**Generalized hypersensitivity syndrome (an early articulation of the central thesis):**

"With long-term trigeminal hypertonicity and elevated substance P levels, you end up with a central hypersensitivity syndrome. About 4-5 studies have been done on this. I've never seen a decent article that knows how to *reverse* it. Reversing requires lining up the bite extremely precisely for a prolonged period — the neuroplasticity effects come in and the body starts calming down.

"These patients become hypersensitive to all sensory input. Hypochondria — illness is not what happens to you; illness is how you react to what happens to you. These people overreact to everything — particularly anything stimulating the trigeminal: dental work, nose surgery, minor whiplash. They go off the deep end. End up with chronic illness. Then any new input puts them into fibromyalgia."

**Why dentistry hasn't caught up:**

"Dentistry doesn't have a good theory of occlusion. They're still just looking at teeth. They don't take enough information into consideration. The instrumentation is not precise enough. In hypersensitive people, anesthetic doesn't work — they just keep giving them shots."

**A self-experiment moment from the lecture (memorable):**

"One time I was in a chair and they used a slow-speed drill on me with real heavy vibration. I momentarily went unconscious. I was out for about 3 seconds. I can feel myself go down and come back again. How did that vibration cause that? How does a person go unconscious when a boxer hits them in the jaw? Too strong trigeminal sensory input."

**On the carbon-paper problem:**

"Most dentists use carbon paper to check how you bite. Doesn't work on hypersensitive cases. We use polyvinyl siloxane — a silicone material we squirt in their mouth, sets in seconds. Three-dimensional pressure gauge. ~30× more precise than carbon paper."

## Q&A — Treatment cost & timeline

- Young children for orthodontic purposes without major medical complaints: **$2,500-3,500**
- Adults with severe medical complaints: up to **$6,000-8,000**
- Phase 1 (find where the jaw wants to be) → Phase 2 (use the same appliance to move teeth into the position to support it)
- Can take from 1.5 years up to 5+ years

## Q&A — Are there "normal" people?

"Almost none. Probably 3% or less. And those few who are functionally ideal would be considered *dentally abnormal* by orthodontists."

## Q&A — Sinus / autoimmune / ear

"All the autonomics to your sinus travel with the trigeminal. You see chronic sinusitis with jaw dysfunction all the time. The TMJ patients I treated went to the ENT 10 times more often than the population at large.

"Most of my MS cases I think we're looking at long-term chronic vascular insufficiency from trigeminal dysfunction. Some of these may explain a lot of the neuropathies — the numbness, tingling, weakness. When you align the bite on these patients, there's an immediate increase in circulation to arms and legs — they're stronger. You can muscle-test them. They're immediately stronger when you get that bite lined up."

## Q&A — Wisdom teeth

"You'd want to keep wisdom teeth if at all possible. But we've degenerated to the point that more than two-thirds of us don't have room enough to keep them. Dentists don't like to work that far back, so they tend to recommend they all be pulled. But you'd want to keep them if at all possible — there's a lot of support that far back."
