This is essentially the same content as the Parkinson's Resource Org video — Dr. Jennings explaining the four mechanisms by which jaw alignment dysfunction relates to Parkinson's:

1. **The jaw joint is compressed too far back in the skull** — visible on imaging. Normally the space behind the condyle should be larger than the space in front.

2. **Patients are "a-gape"** — jaw falls too far away from upper teeth at rest. Causes a hollow-sounding voice or lisp.

3. **Rigidity:** The Japanese have done 35 years of research showing that anytime teeth are shortened on one side, it induces increased hypertonicity in the muscles on the opposite side of the body. Strong evidence that rigidity can be induced by a bite being off.

4. **Abnormal blink reflex** (in 70% of Parkinson's patients): The blink reflex is modulated by the trigeminal nerve (sensory in) and the seventh facial nerve (motor out). With bite dysfunction, the trigeminal sensory is disturbed, altering blink reflex.

5. **Loss of smell:** Two "noses" — olfactory (first cranial nerve) and the chemoreceptors controlled by the trigeminal nerve. With trigeminal disturbance, you often see shifting smell characteristics — from multiple chemical sensitivity to loss of smell. Dr. Jennings: "I've had many cases in which our therapy has restored smell after being missing for many years."

6. **Movement origination via the reticular formation:** Known since 1905 that stimulating the reticular formation induces tremors and parkinsonian movements. Research shows jaw alignment sensors go through the trigeminal into the trigeminal mesencephalic nucleus → second-order neurons into the reticular formation. Those are the predominant most populous cells in the reticular formation.

**Typical treatment:** An orthotic that covers the teeth and fills up the a-gape-ness, restoring jaw alignment through positioning.
