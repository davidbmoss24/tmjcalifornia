A short walkthrough of what an appointment looks like — Dr. Jennings demonstrating his appliances and the silicone-bite technique.

Dr. Jennings shows the **twin block** appliance on Joel: a lower piece that clips onto the teeth, an upper piece with two plastic ramps that just miss each other so the jaw is stimulated to bite forward.

To check the bite: silicone epoxy that sets up in about 20 seconds, squirted over the teeth. Most dentists use carbon paper for this; this material is **far more precise**, ~30× more accurate. Held up to the light, the imprint shows precisely how the teeth come together. It's three-dimensional — gives thickness like a pressure gauge. Carbon paper shows you *where* you hit; this shows *how hard* and *what part* of the opposing tooth is causing the hitting.

If the imprint shows equal amounts of light coming through on both sides — top and ramps — the bite is even. If not, they drill right through the blue goop, exactly where the defect is.

Joel: "I'm sure in the beginning you had to really persist. But now you just look at it and you can see millimeters by eye."

**On the appliances vs. braces:**

The appliances grab lower on the tooth. When you move the teeth, they move bodily — the higher up you grab a tooth, the more it tips without bodily moving. These appliances were designed to make jaws grow wider.

> "Where we put that lower jaw is where the jaw hinges on a natural arc. We're trying to find that natural arc of opening and closing. When you're trying to figure out where a jaw should be, it's not best to look at where you close to — which is where orthodontists start from. They put your teeth together and diagnose you. We look at where you *open* in diagnosis. Where you go in space as you open dictates where you're going to be when you close."

> "You can't get back teeth taller with braces. You have to have plastic in between the teeth to support the jaw, make room for those teeth to grow. If teeth are always touching, they won't grow."

Treatment in Dr. Jennings' practice is **segmental orthodontics** — treating one segment at a time. Back first, then front, then eventually the middle.
